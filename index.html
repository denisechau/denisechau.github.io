Umap

The Challenge

UC San Diego's main campus is a tough space to navigate for students and visitors alike. The area is vast and the built environment is complex. As a result of the university's gradual growth, the dispersal of buildings, departments, and pathways is rather arbitrary.

To alleviate issues caused by aforementioned characteristics, we focus on wayfinding, information systems designed to guide people through a physical environment and enhance their understanding of a space.

Erik Jepsen, UC San Diego Publications

What's the big deal?

Effective wayfinding systems enhance the way people experience space by making them feel empowered and secure in unfamiliar, high stress environments.

The Discovery

After identifying our general problem statement, we began conducting research to further explore user needs, attitude, and behavior.

By the end of our research phase, we will have conducted two distinct rounds. Research efforts were split evenly amongst all nine members of the team.

Data Collection: Round One

Objectives

How are resources such as digital maps, physical maps, signage, and verbal instruction used?

When do users prefer which medium and why?

What user groups prefer which medium? Are certain groups more successful with particular mediums?

How have past experiences navigating on campus influenced current attitude and behavior?

Methods

contextual inquiry with participants as they tried navigating to unfamiliar destinations from Library Walk

post-task interview about previous use cases or related experiences

Findings

  • most users first search on a mobile Internet browser to see their destination on a map or to read a description of the location
  • if users became confused en route and knew they were in the viscinity of their destination, most then asked a passerby for directions
  • digital resources (maps, department and student service websites) are typically referenced before a trip so users can plan ahead accordingly
  • printed and stationary maps are supplementary at best, but rarely preferred because they lack sufficient details
  • signage with the building name is crucial for users to identify a building confidently
  • wifi and cell service are spotty
This map is posted throughout campus and is distributed at well-attended events and offices, yet several major walkways (orange) are omitted

In our analysis of round one, we noticed that directions to a destination seemed easiest to explain in relation to a nearby landmark in that viscinity, leading us to conduct one more study.

Data Collection: Round Two

Objectives

What are the most effective ways to explain how to get somewhere, assuming that the starting point is Library Walk?

What are the most common landmarks referenced for each college?

The western half of campus is on a cardinal grid. Are cardinal points used in explanation?

Methods

asked participants to give directions to 14 locations from Library Walk and recorded their answers; these locations were distributed as evenly as possible (2 in each college, 2 in University Center)

Findings

  • directions often included the college name (UCSD is divided into six distinct colleges) and one or more landmarks
  • it was common for participants to explain the outward appearance of a landmark
  • although half the campus is on a cardinal grid, cardinal points weren't used often
Frequently referenced landmarks during data collection round two (middle row)

The Refinement

Whew, that was a lot of data. Now what?

As all great UX-ers do, we created an affinity diagram to organize and group our findings. These are the needs and insights we decided to focus on:

  • users need a wayfinding resource on campus that does not depend on wifi/cellular data
  • the most effective directions are anchored against nearby landmarks, especially because a long set of directions can be difficult to remember
  • users need just enough detail to determine if their trip is worth making; nobody wants to embark on a 20 minute walk only to realize they've arrived at the wrong building or that the office is closed
  • users need to see an image of the building to be confident that they've arrived at the correct place

We all know how easy it is to become distracted with solving edge cases, so we created personas to keep us grounded in realistic use cases.

The Busy Student

Ada is a third year undergrad majoring in Psychology. She lives off campus, works part time at a market on campus and is highly involved in several student associations, including AS Concerts and Events and Kaibigang Pilipin.

Ada's main concern is being punctual. Her most common struggles are locating TA section rooms at the start of a quarter and finding meeting rooms for her student organizations.

As an activities coordinator for KP, it's not unusual to host events with other KP chapters. In the past, Ada has had a difficult time directing visitors to appropriate parking lots.

The Occasional Visitor

Jason is the owner of a used bookstore in Hillcrest. He has a community borrowing card for the UCSD library system and likes to check out the special collections archive for new poetry.

On average, Jason visits 3-4 times a year. He typically drives to campus and parks at the Gilman Structure because it's easy to find from the freeway exit. Once on campus, he tends to spend the entire day between the library and dining halls or coffee shops.

The Vision

The intent is to provide touch surface kiosks near major landmarks and walkways throughout campus to assist in the overall planning and execution of travels by foot.

The Wireframes

Spatial orientation can be challenging when you're simultaneously trying to identify buildings around you and remember a set of instructions. To make matters less complicated, match the user's orientation with that of the map.

For example, if you are facing Geisel Library while using the kiosk, it will appear above you on the map (left). Similarly, if you are facing west, Geisel will be to your left on the map (right).

The course and parking widgets are reference guides and a navigational tool all at once.

It's the combination of description and imagery that makes Umap a powerful resource.

Each set of directions would be given alongside annotated imagery to help illustrate what the actual journey looks like. It's an effective way to give directions in a space that has highly unique structures and unnamed walkways. Some examples:

walk toward Geisel and turn left into the alley adjacent to Fairbanks coffee

continue straight then turn right into Conrad Prebys main entrance

Physical Considerations

Digital screens tend to be hard to see in direct sunlight and San Diego is indeed a very sunny place. It'd be best if the kiosk provided shade for the screen and perhaps the user as well.

The screen's angle and height should be adjustable to make it more accessible for those with a handicap.

Closing Thoughts

On Experimental Design

Define very specific everything — hypotheses, methods, and measures — to collect comparable and actionable data.

It's imperative that research experiments are executed consistently to promote measurability. In part, this circles back to my "specific everything" point but also to delegating tasks responsibly and leaving research up to a small number of teammates. Each researcher has a subtly different style in speaking, gesturing, and overall interaction. With fewer researchers, there's less variability in how methods are carried out, especially when the method itself is open-ended, as is with contextual inquiry and ethnographic observation.

On Collaboration

The team became more effective once we broke out into smaller groups working toward specific deliverables and goals. I noticed that the level of engagement had risen, along with accountability and overall morale. My takeaway is that it's crucial to keep the goals focused and achievable so everyone feels one step closer to an end product after each small victory.
